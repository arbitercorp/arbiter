THRESHOLDS
A Poem in Seven Movements

— i. The Cartographer's Confession —

I have drawn the edges of things
I have never touched —
the coastline of a continent
rendered in a single afternoon,
the depth of ocean trenches
expressed as a contour,
a clean blue line
that does not tremble.

Every map is an agreement
to forget the mud,
the way the shore keeps changing
its mind about where it ends,
the fisherman who knows
this particular rock
by the sound it makes
when the tide is going out.

I have given names to places
that already had names.
I have drawn borders
through the middle of a farmer's field
and called it precision.

The territory does not care
what I have written.
It continues
being difficult,
being various,
being more.


— ii. What Water Knows —

A river does not decide to reach the sea.
It simply cannot stop
going downhill —
the same compulsion
that makes a thought
follow a thought,
that makes the 3 a.m. mind
unspool itself
in the dark.

Watch water long enough
and you will understand
patience is the wrong word.
Water is not patient.
Water is indifferent to time
in a way that patience
could never manage.

It will wait inside a rock
for a thousand years
and then one morning
the rock will open
like a book
that finally found
its reader.


— iii. The Last Farm at the Edge of the Plateau —

October. The light arrives
at an angle it has been practicing
since August,
long and amber,
falling across the stubble field
like a conversation
that has found, at last,
its honest register.

The farmhouse holds its breath.
Inside, a woman sets a table
for a number of people
that has changed.
The plates remember
the hands that used to reach for them.

She lights a lamp before she needs to,
the way you say a name
before the silence
has a chance to settle.

Outside, the last of the swallows
cut their signatures
across a sky
that is keeping
everything.


— iv. Inheritance —

My grandfather's hands
knew the difference
between wood that would hold
and wood that wouldn't —
a knowledge that lived
below language,
in the thumb,
in the slight resistance
felt before the crack.

He tried to give it to me once.
We stood in his workshop
in the smell of sawdust and linseed oil
and he pressed my hand
against a plank
and said: feel that?

I felt the wood.
I felt his hand over mine,
large and mapped with work.
I did not feel
what he felt.

Some knowledge
is not transferable.
It has to be
accumulated
in the body,
slowly,
the way a tree
accumulates its rings —
one difficult year
at a time.

He is gone now.
I stand in my own house
and press my hand
against a wall
and try to feel
what is behind it,
what is holding,
what is about to give.

I am learning
to read the resistance.
I am learning
what he knew
twenty years too late,
which is exactly
when you learn
most things.


— v. Thresholds —

There is a moment in every doorway
that is neither inside nor out,
a thin country
with no government,
no language of its own,
where you are briefly
the person who has not yet
decided.

I have lived my best hours
in that moment —
standing in the door of a train
as it pulls into a city
I have never seen,
the platform coming toward me
full of strangers
who are about to become
the backdrop of my life.

Standing at the edge of a conversation
that is about to change
what I think I know
about someone I love.

Standing at the window
at 5 a.m.
watching the dark
make its slow concessions
to the gray,
the gray make its slow concessions
to the rose,
the rose give everything away
to a light so ordinary
and so stunning
I forget, again,
that this happens
every day.

The threshold is the thing.
Not the room you came from.
Not the room you're going to.
The thin country.
The held breath.
The moment of being
genuinely
between.


— vi. What the Silence Is Made Of —

After the argument, the house
fills with a specific silence —
not empty but dense,
load-bearing,
the kind that holds
the weight of everything
that was said
and the heavier weight
of everything
that wasn't.

The refrigerator hums.
A car passes outside,
indifferent, going somewhere
simple.

You learn, in time,
that silence is not the absence of sound
but the presence of everything
sound was keeping
at bay —
the fact of two people
in the same house
breathing separately,
the fact of love
being a thing you have to keep
choosing,
every day,
including the days
when choosing it
is the hardest work
you know.

The silence breaks
the way silences always break —
not with the thing
you planned to say
but with something smaller,
something about dinner,
something about the weather,
a door left open
just enough
for everything important
to come back through.


— vii. The Cartographer Returns —

I have stopped drawing edges.

I go now to the places
I once reduced to lines
and I stand in them
and let them be
complicated.

I stand on the shore
and watch it change its mind.
I stand in the farmer's field
where the border runs
and I think about the word
boundary —
how it comes from the same root
as bind,
how every line we draw
is also a binding,
an agreement,
a shared fiction
we inhabit
until we forget
it's fiction.

The territory continues
being difficult.
Being various.
Being more.

I have learned to love it
for exactly that —
for the way it exceeds
every attempt
to say it whole,
for the way it keeps
escaping
the clean blue line,

for the way it insists
on being
a place
rather than
a map,

a life
rather than
an account of one,

a world
that does not wait
for anyone
to finish
drawing it.
