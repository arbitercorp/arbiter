# Nuragic Culture: A Reference Overview

## Chronology

Nuragic culture flourished on the island of Sardinia from approximately 1800 BCE to the first centuries CE. The period is divided into four archaeological phases:

- **Middle Bronze Age / Early Nuragic (c. 1800–1350 BCE):** Construction of the earliest nuraghi begins. Material culture shows continuity with the preceding Bonnanaro culture. Single-tower forms dominate.
- **Recent Bronze Age (c. 1350–1200 BCE):** The "golden age" of Nuragic civilisation. Complex multi-tower nuraghi reach their greatest elaboration. Long-distance trade contacts with Mycenaean Greece, Cyprus, and the broader Aegean are attested by imported goods. Nuraghe construction effectively ceases by the end of this phase (~1200 BCE).
- **Final Bronze Age (c. 1200–950 BCE):** A significant reorganisation follows the cessation of nuraghe building. Village settlements expand around existing nuraghi; sacred wells proliferate, suggesting a shift in ritual focus. This transition broadly coincides with the wider Mediterranean disruptions of c. 1200 BCE, though a direct causal link to the Sea Peoples remains speculative and is not established consensus.
- **Early Iron Age (c. 950–720 BCE):** Nuragic communities continue to occupy and ritually reuse older structures. Phoenician traders establish coastal contacts from approximately 900 BCE onward. Formal Nuragic culture is considered to dissolve following Carthaginian political consolidation in the sixth century BCE and Roman annexation in 238 BCE, though rural communities in the Barbagia interior retained elements of pre-Roman practice well into the imperial period.

## The Nuraghe Structures

The defining monument of this civilisation is the **nuraghe** (pl. *nuraghi*) — a truncated conical tower built from large, dry-laid basalt blocks without mortar, with corbel-vaulted internal chambers and spiral internal stairs. Approximately 7,000 nuraghi survive; estimates of the original total range from 10,000 to 30,000, making them among the densest concentrations of prehistoric monuments in Europe.

Structural types include:

- **Protonuraghe (corridor nuraghe):** Earliest form; solid or near-solid core with internal passages but no true chamber.
- **Simple tholos nuraghe:** Single tower with one or more corbelled chambers stacked vertically.
- **Complex nuraghe:** A central tower (the *mastio*) surrounded by a bastioned courtyard and subsidiary towers, sometimes further enclosed by an outer curtain wall. The largest example, **Su Nuraxi di Barumini** — inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1997, and the anchor of a 31-site tentative nomination submitted in 2021 — comprises a four-tower bastion around a central keep, with an extensive surrounding village.

The function of nuraghi remains debated. Current consensus treats them as multi-purpose: residences for élite lineages, centres of local authority, storage facilities, and possibly watch-towers or refuges. Their distribution across the landscape suggests territorial organisation, with each nuraghe commanding a defined agricultural or pastoral zone. No interpretation covers all examples uniformly.

## Society and Economy

Nuragic society was organised around **clan or lineage groups**, each associated with one or more nuraghi. No evidence of a unified state has been identified; the political structure appears to have been a mosaic of chiefdoms, though whether this shaded into proto-state organisation in the peak period is contested. Bronze figurines depicting warriors, archers, chieftains, and boxers confirm a stratified, warrior-dominated society in which martial status and ritual authority were intertwined.

The economy rested on:

- **Pastoralism and agriculture:** Cattle, sheep, goats, and pigs; cultivation of emmer wheat, barley, and legumes.
- **Metallurgy:** Sardinia's deposits of copper, lead, and silver made it a significant producer and exporter. Nuragic bronzesmiths were highly skilled; their output — weapons, tools, and the famous **bronzetti** (small votive figurines) — circulated widely across the Mediterranean.
- **Trade:** Nuragic artefacts have been recovered in mainland Italy, Iberia, France, Greece, Cyprus, and the Levant. Mycenaean and Cypriot goods appear at Nuragic sites from the Recent Bronze Age onward. The scale and mechanisms of this exchange — whether direct voyaging, intermediary networks, or both — remain under active scholarly discussion (see Matta & Vandkilde 2023).

## Religion and Ritual

Nuragic religion is imperfectly understood; no written texts survive, and interpretation depends entirely on material evidence.

**Sacred wells** (*pozzi sacri*) are the most archaeologically distinctive ritual structures: approximately 70 are known, built as stone-lined shafts with corbelled underground chambers, several aligned to admit sunlight at specific solar events. Their proliferation in the Final Bronze Age (post-1200 BCE) suggests they became increasingly central to religious life as nuraghe construction ceased. They are broadly associated with water veneration, possibly connected to fertility or oracular practice.

**Megaron temples** — rectangular buildings with an antechamber and main hall — appear in the later Nuragic period and show possible Aegean formal influence, though the degree of direct cultural borrowing versus independent development is contested.

The **bronzetti** are the richest iconographic source. Thousands of small bronze figurines deposited at sacred sites depict warriors, priests, animals, boats, and hybrid beings. They demonstrate votive practice and hint at a complex pantheon, but no specific deity names are recoverable. Collective ritual feasting is evidenced by faunal assemblages and pottery concentrations at communal gathering sites adjacent to nuraghi.

## Decline and Legacy

Nuragic culture did not end abruptly. Phoenician traders established coastal emporia from approximately 900 BCE; Carthage extended political control over much of Sardinia from the sixth century BCE. Roman conquest in 238 BCE accelerated cultural assimilation, though interior communities maintained elements of pre-Roman practice for generations.

The legacy is substantial. Nuragic bronzetti and architectural remains constitute one of the most distinctive prehistoric traditions in the Mediterranean. Su Nuraxi di Barumini's UNESCO inscription and the broader 2021 tentative nomination reflect sustained international recognition. In contemporary Sardinian identity, the nuraghi function as potent symbols of cultural continuity — a pre-Roman, pre-Phoenician heritage predating the island's recorded history by more than a millennium.
